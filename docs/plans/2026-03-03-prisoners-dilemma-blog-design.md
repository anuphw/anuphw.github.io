# Blog Post Design: Nash Equilibrium & Asymmetric Punishment

**Date:** 2026-03-03
**Status:** Draft — author will revise on local

---

## Core Argument

When punishment is fixed and equal regardless of wealth, a powerful player can trivially
rewrite the cost function of a weaker player — making betrayal a rational dominant strategy
for the weaker player. The Nash equilibrium the justice system depends on breaks down.

This is not a moral argument. It is game theory.

---

## Audience

General readers. No prior knowledge of game theory assumed. Math is illustrative, not
derivation-heavy. Payoff matrices shown as simple tables, not formal notation.

---

## Tone

Analytical and informational. No policy prescription. No named real-world figures.
One closing hint for readers who want to connect the dots themselves.

---

## Structure

### Opening (before Section 1)

Brief motivating paragraph. A powerful person. An incident. Someone else who took the blame.
No names. Plant the question: *why would someone do that?* Then: "Game theory has an answer."

### Section 1 — The Classic Game

- Introduce Salman and Ashok: two people, one crime, interrogated separately
- Four outcomes: both silent, both confess, one betrays the other
- Show symmetric 2x2 payoff matrix (years in prison as cost)
- Explain Nash equilibrium: both confess, even though mutual silence is collectively better
- Key insight: neither is irrational — they're responding to the structure of the game

### Section 2 — What "Rational" Means

- Clarify: game theory defines rationality as minimizing your own cost
- The equilibrium is a property of the payoff matrix, not of the players' character
- Neither Salman nor Ashok is villainous — they're playing the game they're in

**Image prompt:**
> *Flat geometric illustration, warm Indian color palette (saffron, deep teal, terracotta, off-white), clean lines, minimal detail, no text in image, consistent character designs throughout — A simple 2x2 grid drawn as a game board, two chess pieces on opposite corners, numbers in each cell, clean and minimal with one accent color*

### Section 3 — Breaking the Symmetry

- Ask: what if the two players are not equal?
- One player (Salman) has wealth and influence; the other (Ashok) does not
- The powerful player can make promises outside the interrogation room — money, security,
  future for family — that effectively rewrite Ashok's cost of confessing
- Show how the payoff matrix changes: Ashok's "confess" cost drops dramatically
- His rational choice flips: confessing is now the dominant strategy for him
- Key insight: Salman didn't need to threaten anyone. He just needed to make the math work.

**Image prompt:**
> *Flat geometric illustration, warm Indian color palette (saffron, deep teal, terracotta, off-white), clean lines, minimal detail, no text in image, consistent character designs throughout — The same 2x2 payoff matrix, but one side of the board is tipped and tilted by a large heavy weight pressing down on it, one player's numbers falling off the board, dramatic lighting*

### Section 4 — The Hint

- One short paragraph, no names
- A powerful person, an incident, a loyal associate who took the fall
- "The game theory does not judge. It just shows you the math."

**Image prompt:**
