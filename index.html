The Source of Organizational Differences: Organization, Manager and Individual Decision Making [Read full abstract]

  • Joint with Henrich Greve and Nils Rudi
  • Submitted to Organization Science
  • We study one source of cheating by time wasting in Football which is delaying the restart of a game and impact of individual, team and manager characteristics on the decision of when and how much to cheat.

Organizational are thought to evolve to have different routines and skill sets, which in turn explain both behavioral differences and outcome differences. One fundamental difference is in how decisions are made, as organizations may differ both in criteria and approaches, but such differences are difficult to assess directly because researchers rarely have access to sufficiently many decisions made within and across organizations. Using newly available data, we examine strategic time wasting in professional football (soccer), comparing decision-making routines reflecting rational behavior, anchoring and adjustment, and contextual heuristics. We find the most common overall decision-making approach for systematic and strategic time wasting, and but also find that organizations have distinct differences in their decision making. We proceed to drill down to the levels of the team, the manager, and the individual players as sources of these differences. Our findings demonstrate a general approach for analyzing sources of decision-making differences, and show that in this context, players and coaches are the most important sources of organizational decision-making routines, with teams having a weaker effect.

Rational Fouls? Decision-Making Under Pressure [Read full abstract]
[Draft available on request]

  • With Henrich Greve and Nils Rudi
  • Does quality of decisions of expert deteriorate under pressure?


Abstract:

When organizational tasks require rapid and accurate decision making in order to avoid costly failure, it is of interest to examine the quality of decisions in general, and specifically the conditions under which it deteriorates. We take some propositions on individual decision making that have been explored most extensively in experimental studies and investigate them in a high-stakes field context involving teamwork: football (soccer) in a European top division. We examine fouls – behaviors that help the team but are against the rules – and start by checking the fit of a model of rational fouling. Next we examine deviations from this model, and are particularly interested in the effects on decision-making of stress and anger from personal failure. We show that fouls (destructive play) are made rationally overall, and with reductions in rationality that follow the predictions of theory on loss aversion and stress.

Estimation of Retail Store Traffic to Achieve Better Conversion. [Read full abstract]

  • with Serguei Netessine and Nils Rudi
  • What is the right way to measure the traffic and conversion at retail store?


Abstract:

Conversion is one the most important KPI that is used to evaluate retail stores. It influences various decisions like staffing, advertisement expenditure, manager bonuses, etc. We collected detailed behavioral data on customers visiting to multiple retail stores in Europe and Middle-east. The data suggested that, group of customers seldom have multiple transactions and hance the traditional definition of conversion, which is the ratio of #Transactions to #Visitors, underestimates the actual conversion. Moreover, the quality of traditional conversion estimate depends on the mean size of groups visiting the store. In this paper, we mathematically modeled the customer arrival process and came up with five estimation techniques to structurally estimate the #Groups in given stream of customers using only traffic counter data.
