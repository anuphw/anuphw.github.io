> **What this series is — and isn't**
>
> This series does not tell you what the "right" outcome looks like. We are not arguing that more AI in schools is good or bad. We are not saying your kid should or shouldn't use ChatGPT. We are mapping the game being played around your child — who the players are, what they want, and how different choices lead to different outcomes. Different families, societies, and governments will land in different equilibria. Our job is to make the game visible. Your job is to decide which outcome you want your family in — and play accordingly.

You've probably wondered if your child is learning the right things in an AI world. Maybe you've watched them paste an essay prompt into ChatGPT and felt a vague unease you couldn't quite name. Or maybe you've wondered whether the school banning phones is protecting your child or just pretending a technological shift isn't happening. That question — what should my kid actually be learning right now? — is worth taking seriously. Not dismissing with reassurance, and not catastrophizing either.

This guide maps the game. Who is playing, what they want, and what the likely outcomes are depending on how each player moves.

## The Four Players

**Governments** set the rules of education. They decide what gets tested, what gets funded, and whether AI tools are permitted or prohibited in classrooms. But governments are also political animals — they respond to pressure from parents, from teachers' unions, from technology companies, and from whoever is currently winning the culture war. When you see a government flip from "ban AI in schools" to "AI literacy for everyone" in 18 months, that's not incoherence — that's a government responding to shifting political winds. Understanding this means understanding that policy is not a neutral technocratic exercise. It is the output of a game.

**Schools** are caught between three pressures at once: their reputation (which depends on outcomes that may or may not reflect actual learning), compliance with whatever the government has mandated, and simple resource efficiency. A school administrator worrying about teacher burnout, per-pupil funding, and next year's test scores is not in a good position to be a bold innovator. Most schools move cautiously. They adopt AI tools when they feel safe to do so and resist when the political or reputational risk feels high. This is rational, even when it frustrates parents who want more.

**Parents** are making investment decisions — about time, money, tutoring, supplemental technology access, and which credentials they believe will matter when their child enters the workforce in fifteen years. High-resource parents can supplement whatever the school provides; lower-resource parents are more dependent on what the school gets right. Both groups are guessing about an uncertain future, but they're guessing from very different starting positions.

**Students** are mostly reactive to the environment everyone else creates. The student who uses AI thoughtfully is often doing so because a parent or teacher made that possible and modeled how. The student who outsources every assignment to a chatbot is often doing so because nobody in their environment has made the alternative feel worth it. There are motivated outliers — students who develop genuine curiosity and judgment regardless of their environment — but designing a system around outliers is not policy.

## Three Tensions You've Probably Argued About at Dinner

These aren't hypothetical debates. They're the live fault lines in education systems right now, in almost every country simultaneously. Each one is explored in depth in this series.

**"Should my kid use ChatGPT for homework or figure it out themselves?"**

This is the dependency vs. capability tension. Using AI to get answers faster might be building a skill (working with AI tools) or it might be atrophying a skill (the ability to reason through hard problems without assistance). Both things can be true at the same time, for different students, in different subjects. The question worth asking isn't "is AI bad" but "what is my child actually developing capacity for?" [Post 1: The Classroom Game](/blog/2026/llm-education-1-classroom-game/) maps this formally.

**"Is my kid falling behind because we can't afford the best AI tools?"**

This is the equity vs. efficiency tension. AI tutoring tools can be genuinely effective — but access is uneven. When high-resource families can supplement school with expensive private AI tutors and low-resource families can't, any efficiency gains from AI in education can widen the gap they were supposed to close. [Post 2: The Evolving Game](/blog/2026/llm-education-2-evolving-game/) shows how this plays out over time.

**"Should schools ban phones and AI, or embrace them?"**

This is the autonomy vs. control tension. Prohibition is easy to implement and easy to defend politically. It is also, in the medium term, probably futile — students will have AI access outside school regardless. The question is whether schools engage with that reality or pretend it doesn't exist. [Post 3: The Continuous Theory](/blog/2026/llm-education-3-continuous-theory/) examines how the mix of parents and schools in a district changes which answer a government will land on.

## What Skills Might Still Matter — Conditional Hypotheses, Not Predictions

We are not fortune tellers. Every claim below is a conditional — "if X, then Y." The "if" matters as much as the "then." Anyone claiming certainty about which skills will matter in 20 years is guessing.

It helps to think of skills on a spectrum:

**Fully substitutable** — things AI can already do better and cheaper than humans, where human performance provides no premium. Basic data entry, certain forms of translation, some kinds of writing where quality is judged by speed and correctness alone.

**Contested** — things where AI is capable but human performance still commands a premium in some contexts, and we genuinely don't know if that premium will persist. Code review, legal drafting, therapy. These might land anywhere.

**AI-amplified** — things where combining human judgment with AI tools produces better outcomes than either alone. Research, complex strategy, most kinds of creative direction. The humans who learn to use AI well here will likely outperform those who don't.

**Structurally human** — things where the value is in the fact that a human did it. A friend listening to your problems. A parent at a school play. Perhaps: a student wrestling with a hard problem themselves rather than delegating it.

What about acting, singing, and sports? These are genuinely open questions. One scenario: AI-generated entertainment gets so good and cheap that human performance commands only a nostalgia premium. Another scenario: precisely because AI-generated content becomes ubiquitous, live human performance becomes a luxury good valued more than ever. We don't know. Anyone telling you with certainty which skills will matter in 20 years is guessing. We prefer to show you the map of possibilities.

## What the LLM Trajectory Means for a Child Starting School Today

Here is what the evidence suggests about where AI capability is right now:

AI is currently slowing down. The massive gains from 2020 to 2023 have been followed by a period of diminishing returns. Models are getting incrementally better but not transformatively better. We appear to be approaching a capability plateau — a period of relative stability.

A breakthrough is coming. The history of technology says so, even if we can't say when. A new architecture, a new training approach, a new integration of AI with robotics or biology — something will restart the exponential. We don't know if that's two years away or twelve.

This matters for your child because the equilibria being formed right now — the habits, skills, and institutional arrangements settling into place — may shatter when the next breakthrough arrives. A school system built around "AI does the writing, humans do the evaluating" could look very different after a breakthrough that makes evaluation itself AI-capable. [Post 4: The Full Picture](/blog/2026/llm-education-4-full-picture/) explores which of the equilibria we're building are resilient to shocks and which are fragile.

## Five Things Any Parent Can Think About

These are not prescriptions. Every family's situation is different. These are questions worth sitting with.

1. **What ratio of AI use to independent effort makes sense for your child's learning style?** The answer probably isn't zero and probably isn't one hundred percent. Where is the line for your child, in which subjects, at which ages?

2. **What credentials will employers value in 15 years — and is your government's education policy tracking that?** The answer to the second question is probably no, not because governments are incompetent but because nobody knows the answer to the first question with confidence.

3. **Is your child developing curiosity and judgment, or just output optimization?** A student who has learned to produce acceptable essays quickly is not the same as a student who has learned to think. Both might get the same grade right now.

4. **Does your school's assessment system detect whether your child is actually learning?** If assessments can be passed with AI assistance and the school hasn't accounted for that, the credential may be drifting from the underlying skill it's supposed to represent.

5. **Are you making active choices about AI access in your household, or letting defaults decide?** The defaults — whatever apps are installed, whatever the school permits, whatever your child's friends are using — are choices too. They're just someone else's choices.

## The Game Is Being Played Regardless

You can opt out of thinking about this. Many parents do. The game keeps running. Governments keep setting policies, schools keep adapting (or not), other parents keep making supplementation decisions that shape the competitive landscape your child is navigating.

Understanding the game doesn't guarantee you'll win it. It does mean you're playing it consciously rather than by accident.
